Major Muscles

Pectoralis Major

A large chest muscle responsible for pushing movements and bringing the arms toward the body.

Deltoid

The primary shoulder muscle that lifts, rotates, and stabilizes the arm.

Trapezius

A large upper-back muscle that supports posture and controls shoulder movement.

Latissimus Dorsi

A broad back muscle used for pulling movements such as climbing and pull-ups.

Biceps Brachii

A muscle on the front of the upper arm that bends the elbow.

Triceps Brachii

A muscle on the back of the upper arm that straightens the elbow.

Rectus Abdominis

The abdominal muscle commonly known as the "six-pack."

Quadriceps

A group of four muscles that extend the knee and generate power during running and jumping.

Hamstrings

A group of muscles on the back of the thigh that bend the knee and extend the hip.

Gastrocnemius

The major calf muscle responsible for pushing the body forward during movement.

Connective Tissues

Tendon

A strong connective tissue that attaches muscle to bone.

Ligament

A tough connective tissue that connects bone to bone and stabilizes joints.

Fascia

A thin layer of connective tissue that surrounds and supports muscles and organs.

Movement Terms

Flexion

A movement that decreases the angle between two body parts.

Extension

A movement that increases the angle between two body parts.

Abduction

Movement away from the body's midline.

Adduction

Movement toward the body's midline.

Rotation

The turning of a body part around its axis.

Muscle Physiology

Skeletal Muscle

Voluntary muscle tissue attached to bones that allows movement.

Smooth Muscle

Involuntary muscle tissue found in organs and blood vessels.

Cardiac Muscle

Specialized muscle tissue found only in the heart.

Muscle Fiber

A single muscle cell capable of contraction.

Sarcomere

The smallest functional unit responsible for muscle contraction.

Myofibril

A threadlike structure inside muscle fibers that contains the proteins needed for contraction.

Exercise Physiology

ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate)

The main energy source used by muscle cells. ATP provides the energy needed for muscle contractions, movement, and athletic performance.

Aerobic Respiration

The process of producing ATP using oxygen. It supplies energy efficiently during long-duration activities such as running, swimming, and cycling.

Anaerobic Respiration

The production of ATP without oxygen. It provides energy quickly during high-intensity activities but can contribute to fatigue.

Lactate

A by-product produced during intense exercise when oxygen supply cannot meet energy demand. High levels are associated with muscle fatigue.

Muscle Fatigue

A decrease in the muscle's ability to generate force. Fatigue can occur when ATP decreases, lactate accumulates, or energy demands exceed supply.

Oxygen Debt

The additional oxygen required after exercise to restore normal ATP levels and remove metabolic by-products.

Hypertrophy

An increase in muscle size caused by resistance training and muscle fiber adaptation during recovery.

Protein Synthesis

The process of building new proteins inside muscle cells. It helps repair and strengthen muscles after exercise.

Mitochondria

Organelles inside cells that produce ATP through aerobic respiration. Endurance athletes often develop more mitochondria.

Muscle Contraction & Fibers

Actin

A thin protein filament found inside muscle fibers. Actin interacts with myosin to produce muscle contraction.

Myosin

A thick protein filament that pulls on actin during muscle contraction, generating force and movement.

Cross-Bridge Cycling

The repeated interaction between actin and myosin that allows muscles to shorten and produce force.

Motor Neuron

A nerve cell that sends signals from the nervous system to muscle fibers, causing them to contract.

Motor Unit

A motor neuron and all the muscle fibers it controls. Larger motor units generally produce greater force.

Concentric Contraction

A muscle contraction in which the muscle shortens while producing force, such as lifting a dumbbell during a curl.

Eccentric Contraction

A muscle contraction in which the muscle lengthens while resisting force, such as lowering a dumbbell during a curl.

Isometric Contraction

A contraction in which the muscle produces force without changing length, such as holding a plank position.

Type I Muscle Fibers

Slow-twitch fibers that use oxygen efficiently and resist fatigue. They are important for endurance activities.

Type II Muscle Fibers

Fast-twitch fibers that generate powerful contractions and are important for sprinting, jumping, and explosive movements.

Neuromuscular Coordination

The ability of the nervous system and muscles to work together efficiently to produce accurate movement.
